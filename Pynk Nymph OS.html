Brand · Foundation

What We
Believe.

The beliefs underneath everything we do — from how we wax to how we post. A manifesto, not a memo. Everything traces back here.

The BeliefsSeven, plus one
01
Body hair is normal.
You don't need to earn care by looking a certain way first. You don't need to apologize for your hair, your skin, your stretch marks, your scars, or your body. That's what we're here for.
02
Everybody deserves care.
Not eventually. Not after they lose weight or become more confident or "fix" something.
03
Comfort shouldn't be a luxury.
A waxing appointment should not require bravery. People deserve to feel welcome, informed, respected, and comfortable from the moment they walk in.
04
Confidence isn't something we sell.
We don't give people confidence. We help create the conditions for it. The confidence was already theirs.
05
Education is part of the service.
People make better decisions when they understand their bodies. Questions are welcome. Curiosity is welcome. Nobody should leave feeling embarrassed for asking.
06
We take the work seriously. We don't take ourselves seriously.
Waxing involves vulnerable moments. Humor helps. Laughter helps. Being human helps.
07
Richmond matters.
Pynk Nymph exists because of the people who support it. We aren't trying to feel like a chain. We're trying to feel like home.
The final belief · Why we exist
We help people feel comfortable in their bodies.
Not eventually. Not when they lose weight. Not when they become more confident. Not when they finally feel "ready." Now.
We just happen to also remove hair.

Natural Blues

Blue is not an official Pynk Nymph brand color. But it shows up everywhere in our photography — and that's intentional.

Blue enters through the world around the brand — not through graphic design. It creates breathing room in an otherwise warm palette and makes imagery feel open, natural, and lived-in.
✓Natural blue use
  • Blue skies and open sky photography
  • Water, rivers, and reflective surfaces
  • Denim clothing in lifestyle shots
  • Cool environmental shadows and reflections
  • Background color in landscape photography
✗Keep blue out of design
  • No blue typography or headlines
  • No blue graphic elements or shapes
  • No blue buttons, badges, or backgrounds
  • Never introduce new blue brand colors
  • Blue should feel discovered, not designed

Marketing · Before You Design

How We Decide
What to Post.

Before you open Canva, know what you're actually saying. The format is the last decision — not the first.

✦ Studio wisdom
The format is the last decision — not the first. Open Canva after you know what you're saying. Never before.
The order that matters
01
Start with one thing to say
If you can't say it in a sentence, the post isn't ready. What's the one true thing this post communicates? That's your starting point.
02
Ask: why does this matter to them?
Not to us — to them. If the answer is "it doesn't really," it's probably an announcement. Announcements are the lowest-value posts. Reframe it as something they actually care about.
03
Share a view, not just a fact
"Here's what we think about this" beats "here's some information." Pynk Nymph has opinions. Use them. People follow people and perspectives — not bulletin boards.
04
Notice, don't announce
"We saw this and loved it" always lands warmer than "ANNOUNCING our new thing." Lead with the interesting part — the observation, the person, the story. The business stuff comes second.
05
Give first, ask second
If the post only benefits Pynk Nymph, it's not ready. Give value — something useful, funny, true, or moving — before you ever mention booking. The ask lands better when people already feel taken care of.
06
Then open Canva
Templates make a good idea look great. They can't rescue a post that has nothing to say. If you know what you're saying, the format decision is usually obvious. Think first, design second.

If you can't say what the post is for in one sentence, it isn't ready.

The rule we don't break — and the reason this page exists before Canva does.
Is it ready?
✓Ready to post
  • You can say the point in one sentence
  • It gives something before it asks for anything
  • It sounds like Pynk Nymph, not a flyer
  • The format serves the idea
✗Not ready yet
  • It's just an announcement with no hook
  • The idea is vague — you're hoping design saves it
  • It's for us, not for them
  • You opened Canva before knowing what you're saying

Community
Content.

Community content isn't random. We post it because it reflects what Pynk Nymph believes. Every community post should answer one question: why are we sharing this?

What community content does
01
Builds trust
Shows we're part of Richmond — not just selling to it. People buy from brands they feel connected to. Community content is how that connection happens.
02
Shows our values
Demonstrates what we stand for beyond waxing. Who we support, what we celebrate, what we care about — all of it communicates the brand before anyone books.
03
Gives value to everyone
A good community post is meaningful even to someone who will never book with us. If it only matters to existing clients, it's not really community content.
04
Earns the ask
Community content is the long game. Every post that gives without asking makes the next booking post land better. Give first. Ask second.
What Richmond means to us
We're not from Richmond.
We're of Richmond.
“The difference between community content and brand PR is simple. One is genuine. The other is a press release with a local tag.”

Pynk Nymph exists in this city. Our clients live here, work here, eat here, celebrate here. When something happens in Richmond that matters to people we care about — we say something. Not to look good. Because it's real to us.

How to Judge a Community Post Idea

Before you post it, ask these questions. If you can't answer them honestly, the post isn't ready.

✓Worth posting
  • We genuinely care about this — it's not forced
  • It connects to Richmond or a real person/moment
  • It tells a story, not just a fact
  • It gives something before it asks for anything
  • It would resonate with someone who's never heard of us
✗Not ready yet
  • It's just a logo on a community moment we didn't touch
  • We're inserting ourselves into something we don't have a real tie to
  • It reads like a press release or announcement
  • It only benefits Pynk Nymph
  • We're doing it because everyone else is
Good vs. Weak — Side by Side

Hover over a card to see its key takeaway. Open “Why this works” for the full breakdown.

Good Story
The story-first post
“This block raised us. Here's who's on it.” — Opens with a story. The brand is secondary. The moment is real, the people are real, and the caption sounds like a person wrote it.
Key takeaway: You're not in the caption. The community is.

The story comes first. The studio comes second — or not at all. This post would be worth reading even if it were posted by someone who'd never heard of Pynk Nymph. That's the bar.

Notice the caption structure: Real moment → real people → real place. No “we're so honored to…” No “we stand with…” Just the story, told simply.

Weak Promotion
The logo drop
“Happy [Holiday]! We stand with [community]!” — This is a placeholder, not a post. It doesn't say anything specific, connect to anything real, or give value to anyone.
Key takeaway: Generic “support” is invisible. Specificity is everything.

The brand is the subject. The community is the backdrop. That's backwards. This post could have been written by anyone, about anyone, on any day.

If you swapped out the holiday or the community name and nothing else changed, it's a logo drop — not community content.

Good Observation
The observation post
“We went to [event] last weekend and noticed something.” — Personal, specific, and curious. It positions Pynk Nymph as a real participant in the community, not a sponsor.
Key takeaway: We were there. We paid attention. That's enough.

The word “noticed” is doing a lot of work here. It signals presence, curiosity, and a point of view — without being preachy or promotional. We went, we saw, we thought about it.

No booking link. No CTA. No brand message. Just a genuine observation from a business that actually showed up.

Weak Promotion
The announcement dressed up as community
“We're SO excited to announce we'll be at [event]! Come see us!” — This is marketing disguised as community. No story, no value, all ask.
Key takeaway: An announcement is not a community post, no matter how excited you are.

“Come see us” is a sales call. There's nothing here for anyone who doesn't already want to buy something. Community content should give before it asks — this post is all ask.

Save the event announcement for a separate promotional post. Don't dress it up as community when it isn't.

Good Participation
The participation post
“We volunteered. We attended. We learned something.” — Community content isn't only observation. It can also come from genuinely participating. Showing up changes how a post feels. The reader can tell the difference between a brand that watched and a brand that was in it.
Key takeaway: You can't fake presence. Real participation earns the post.

There's a category of post that only works if you actually did the thing. “We volunteered at [local event]” only lands if you actually volunteered. There's no shortcut here — and that's the point.

These posts earn disproportionate trust because participation is rare. Anyone can post about a holiday. Fewer businesses actually show up. The ones that do get remembered.

Caption structure: What we did → What we noticed → Why it mattered. That's it. No promotion needed.

How We Decide to Post

Before scheduling anything, run it through these four questions. If you hesitate on more than one, it's not ready.

01
Does this actually matter to our community?
Not to us. To them. Would a client who's never heard of Pynk Nymph care about this? If the only people it matters to are already in the building, it's internal — not community.
02

Presence earns the post. We volunteered, attended, tasted, visited, witnessed. Reporting on something we only read about online is not community content — it's reposting with branding.
03
Can we contribute something meaningful?
A point of view. A story. A connection we can draw that no one else would draw exactly this way. If our contribution is a logo and a caption slot, we haven't contributed anything.
04
Would we still share this if Instagram didn't exist?
The test for authenticity. If the answer is yes — if we'd text a friend, mention it at checkout, bring it up in conversation — it belongs on the feed. If not, it's content strategy, not community.

2–3 intentional posts per month is plenty — more if something genuinely calls for it. Community content is the seasoning, not the main dish. Post when it's real, not when it's scheduled.

Provider Resources · Service Delivery

Service
Delivery.

The goal is simple: make people feel comfortable in their bodies. Everything on this page — the steps, the scripts, the recommendations — exists to support that. Take care of the person in front of you. The rest follows.

The Visit, Start to Finish
Before they arrive
1
Review the form
Do this
  • Know their name before they walk in
  • Know the service they booked
  • Check for medications — retinoids, Accutane, anything that affects the skin
  • Note any allergies or previous reactions
  • Flag if they're a first-timer so you're ready to walk them through it
During the visit
2
Set the tone
Do this
  • Greet them by name immediately
  • Make eye contact, be unhurried — not rushed
  • Zero judgment energy from the first second
Say this
"First time? You're in good company — I'll walk you through everything."
"Come on back — I've got everything set up for you."
3
Consult — ask and look
Do this
  • While prepping, observe: texture, ingrown tendency, dryness, irritation, tone
  • Let what you see guide what you recommend later — don't pitch cold
Say this
"Anything bugging you about your skin lately?"
"Have you had any irritation or reactions after waxing before?"
4
Narrate the care
Do this
  • Talk through what you see as you work — don't go silent
  • Name the concern, make it sound normal, and plant the solution
  • You're not pitching; you're informing — the recommendation comes later because you set it up now
Say this
"You get a little ingrown action here — totally normal, there's an easy fix."
"Your skin is a bit dry today. That's really common, especially this time of year."
"I'm going to use a soothing wax here — your skin reacts quickly."
Wrapping up
5
Fill out the aftercare card together
Do this
  • Write their service and the date
  • Add 1–2 specific notes written for THEM, not generic ("exfoliate Thursday, not before")
  • Circle the products you'd actually use on their skin — make it feel personal
Example notes by concern
  • Ingrowns: "Exfoliate Thurs — not before. Try the serum on bumps, don't pick them."
  • Dryness: "Moisturize daily — especially the next 48h. Body oil is your friend."
  • Sensitivity: "Skip the hot shower tonight. Cool compress if it's still red tomorrow."
  • Hyperpigmentation: "SPF every morning on exposed areas. Brightening serum at night."
  • First-timer: "You did amazing. Exfoliate at 48h — it gets easier every time."
Say this
"I'm going to write down a couple of notes specifically for your skin."
6
Goodie bag & soft checkout
Do this
  • Walk them through what's in the bag — don't just hand it over
  • First-time clients get a goodie bag with a sample oil and scrub, plus their aftercare card — reference the card as you hand it over
  • Connect any product directly to what you showed them during service
  • Offer the full-size product once. Soft. If they say no, that's fine.
Say this
"I put the ingrown serum in there — that's the one for what I showed you."
"Want me to add it so you're set for the next few weeks?"
After they leave
7
Follow up within the week
Do this
  • Mangomint sends the automated check-in — watch for replies
  • You serviced them, so you respond — not a manager, not a template
  • Answer personally within a few hours of seeing the reply
Say this
"How's the skin settling? Any questions from your appointment?"
Skin & Conditions

Knowing what to look for is its own skill. The full observation guide — concerns, conditions, recommendations, and photo references — lives on the Reading Skin & Hair page. Link from here when you're done with a client visit.

The follow-up, automated

The post-service check-in is automated in Mangomint and fires within a week. But automation only sends it — the provider who did the service is the one who responds. Watch for replies and answer them personally. A real human reply within a few hours is what turns a one-time client into a regular.

Skin &
Conditions.

What to look for on every client — and how what you see tells you exactly what to recommend. Every recommendation traces back to something you observed. Drop reference photos onto any frame.

Photo diversity matters

Each photo slot needs reference images showing the condition on multiple skin tones — not just lighter skin. Conditions like redness, eczema, and folliculitis present very differently on darker skin. Good sources: DermNet NZ (dermnetnz.org), Brown Skin Matters (brownskinmatters.com), and the Skin of Color Society image library. Drop diverse examples onto each frame so every provider on the team learns to recognize conditions accurately.

Ingrown hairs / bumps
Look for
Raised bumps, trapped hairs, post-wax irritation at wax sites.
Recommend
Exfoliating serum (BHA/AHA) + gentle dry brushing.
Routine
Exfoliate 48h after — never day-of. Consistent is better than aggressive.
Lighter skin
Red or pink bumps, sometimes with a visible trapped hair beneath the surface.
Darker skin
Dark brown or purple bumps, or hyperpigmented spots. May present as raised texture without any visible redness — rely on feel and client feedback.
Dryness / flaking
Look for
Dull texture, tightness, visible flaking at or around the wax site.
Recommend
Fragrance-free moisturizer or body oil.
Routine
Hydrate daily — especially the 48h after service.
Lighter skin
White or pink flaking, visible dullness.
Darker skin
Ashy or grayish appearance. Flaking may be subtle — easier to feel than see. Run the back of your hand across the skin to check.
Sensitivity / redness
Look for
Reactive skin during or after service. History of irritation on the intake form.
Recommend
Soothing aloe-based calming gel.
Routine
Cool compress day-of, no hot showers for 24h, skip tight clothing.
Lighter skin
Visible flushing or redness during service.
Darker skin
Redness may not be visible at all. Feel for warmth, watch for swelling, and always ask "how does that feel?" Never rely on color alone to assess sensitivity.
Hyperpigmentation
Look for
Patchy uneven tone, darkening at frequently-waxed areas, post-inflammatory marks.
Recommend
Brightening serum + daily broad-spectrum SPF.
Routine
SPF every morning on exposed areas, brightening serum at night. Consistency matters more than strength.
Lighter skin
Pink or light brown PIH marks that typically fade within weeks.
Darker skin
Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is significantly more pronounced and longer-lasting — what fades in weeks on lighter skin can take months. Treat proactively, not reactively. Niacinamide is particularly effective.
Folliculitis
Look for
Small pustules around hair follicles, warm to touch, clustered pattern.
Recommend
Do not wax over active folliculitis. Refer to a dermatologist if persistent.
Do this
Antibacterial wash, breathable clothing, consistent exfoliation once healed.
Lighter skin
Red pustules or pink-ringed bumps around follicles.
Darker skin
Pustules may appear dark or hyperpigmented rather than red. Surrounding skin may darken even after healing.
Keratosis Pilaris (KP)
Look for
Rough, bumpy texture — usually arms or thighs. Often mistaken for ingrowns.
Recommend
AHA/urea-based lotion — not a standard exfoliant. Can still wax.
Routine
KP doesn't go away — it's managed. Set realistic expectations.
Lighter skin
Small red or skin-colored bumps giving skin a rough, sandpaper feel.
Darker skin
Bumps may appear darker brown or match surrounding skin tone — the telltale sign is texture, not color. Ashy appearance may also be present.
Contact dermatitis
Look for
Red, itchy, inflamed patch — often from a product reaction, not the wax itself.
Recommend
Patch-test wax on a small area. Do not wax over active irritation.
Routine
Fragrance-free everything until resolved. Note on their card.
Lighter skin
Pink or red, well-defined inflamed patch.
Darker skin
May appear as a darker, purplish, or hyperpigmented patch rather than red. Ask about itch and burning — sensation is more reliable than color.
Stretch marks
Look for
Silver or red striations — common at inner thighs, abdomen, hips.
Recommend
Can wax over them — be gentle and pull skin taut. Avoid if raised or textured.
Note
Never comment on them unprompted. Zero judgment.
Lighter skin
Early marks appear pink or red; older marks are silver-white.
Darker skin
Early marks may appear darker brown or purple; older marks often appear lighter than surrounding skin, which can make them less visible. Feel for texture change.
Conditions to Know

These aren't wax-specific concerns — they're conditions that show up on clients and affect how you approach the service. You don't diagnose, but you need to know when to adjust or refer out. Visual cues are listed where there's something to look for.

⚠ High Alert
Retinoid / Retinol Use
Look for: Client mentions tretinoin, Retin-A, or "prescription skin stuff" — or the intake form flags it.

Clients on prescription retinoids: do not wax the face — skin lifts. OTC retinol users: pause 5–7 days before a facial wax. Always check the form.
⚠ High Alert
Accutane (Isotretinoin)
Look for: Listed on the intake form. Ask directly if it looks blank.

Do not wax anyone currently on Accutane or who finished within 6 months. Skin is extremely fragile and will lift. Non-negotiable.
⚠ High Alert
Antibiotics
Look for: Client mentions they're on antibiotics — especially doxycycline, tetracycline, or other acne antibiotics.

Certain antibiotics increase skin sensitivity and can cause more irritation, redness, or lifting than usual. Proceed with caution — use a sensitive wax and give thorough aftercare instructions. Note it on their card.
⚠ Do Not Wax
Hidradenitis Suppurativa (HS)
Look for: Deep, painful lumps or nodules in the groin, inner thigh, or underarms. May resemble recurring boils or cysts. Scarring or tunnel-like tracts in chronic cases.

Do not wax over or near active HS lesions — waxing can traumatize the skin and trigger new flares. If unsure whether a lump is HS or a regular ingrown, don't wax it. Refer out. If in remission in the service area, consult carefully before proceeding.
Lighter skin
Surrounding skin may appear red or inflamed around active lesions.
Darker skin
Surrounding skin often shows significant hyperpigmentation or purple discoloration — even between flares. The hyperpigmentation may make it harder to spot active areas.
⚠ Do Not Wax
Sunburn
Look for: Obvious visual — pink/red, warm, possibly peeling skin.

Do not wax sunburned skin, period. Even mild sunburn means the skin barrier is compromised. Reschedule and let it heal fully — typically at least a week.
⚠ Do Not Wax
Active Wound / Cut / Open Skin
Look for: Any break in the skin — cut, scrape, abrasion, or active breakout with broken skin.

Skip the area entirely. Never wax over broken skin. Document on their card and let them know to come in healed next time.
⚠ Do Not Wax
Tinea (Ringworm / Fungal Infection)
Look for: A ring-shaped, scaly, slightly raised patch — often with a clearer center. May appear anywhere on the body.

Do not wax over active tinea — the wax strip can spread the fungal infection to adjacent skin. Refer out and reschedule when fully treated.
Lighter skin
Appears as a clearly defined pink or red ring.
Darker skin
The ring outline may appear darker brown or hyperpigmented rather than red. The center may look ashy or lighter than surrounding skin.
⚠ Do Not Wax
Active Herpes Simplex (HSV) Outbreak
Look for: Active blisters or sores — typically in clusters, may tingle or burn before they appear.

Never wax over an active HSV outbreak. For clients with HSV history in the service area (e.g., genital HSV and Brazilian), proceed with extra care between outbreaks — some providers recommend a medical consult. Always note HSV history on their card.
⚠ Do Not Wax
Warts / Molluscum Contagiosum
Look for: Small, firm, raised bumps — warts have a rough surface; molluscum are smooth and flesh-colored with a dimple.

Do not wax over warts or molluscum — the friction can spread them to adjacent skin. Skip the area and refer to a dermatologist.
Lighter skin
Appear as skin-colored or slightly pink bumps.
Darker skin
May appear darker or hyperpigmented, and can be easier to miss on deeper skin tones. Feel for texture change.
⚠ Caution
Varicose / Spider Veins
Look for: Visible raised, rope-like veins (varicose) or small spider-web clusters (spider veins) — most common on legs.

Do not wax directly over raised varicose veins — the trauma can aggravate them. Spider veins are generally fine to wax over. When in doubt, wax around, not over.
Condition
Eczema
Look for: Dry, scaly patches — often with rough or cracked texture. Common at inner elbows, behind knees, wrists.

Can wax around (not over) active patches. Flare-ups = skip that area. Between flares, use sensitive wax and go slow. Note on their card.
Lighter skin
Pink or red patches with visible scaling.
Darker skin
Patches appear gray, ashy, dark brown, or purple rather than red. May be harder to spot visually — feel for rough texture and ask if an area is itchy or irritated.
Condition
Psoriasis
Look for: Thick scales on raised patches with well-defined edges. Common on knees, elbows, lower back.

Do not wax over active plaques — trauma can trigger a Koebner response. Wax unaffected areas normally. Always ask about current flare status.
Lighter skin
Silver-white scales on a pink or red base.
Darker skin
Scales may appear gray or white but the underlying skin is more likely dark brown or violet rather than red. Post-flare hyperpigmentation is more significant and longer-lasting.
Condition
Scar Tissue (Surgical / Injury)
Look for: Shiny, smooth, or raised skin — often a different texture or tone than surrounding skin. Common areas: C-section scar (lower abdomen), tummy tuck scar (lower abdomen/hip line), injury scars.

Can generally wax over healed scar tissue, but the skin may be more sensitive and hair growth patterns can change. Be gentle. For C-section or tummy tuck scars: make sure they're fully healed (typically 12+ months post-surgery) and check with the client about their comfort. Never wax over a fresh or raised hypertrophic scar.
Condition
Cellulitis (Skin Infection)
Look for: Red, warm, swollen area — often on the legs. May feel hot to the touch and have a blurry, non-defined border.

Do not wax over active cellulitis — it is a bacterial skin infection requiring medical treatment. If you spot this and the client hasn't been treated, refer them out immediately.
Condition
Diabetes
Look for: No specific visual cue — check the intake form.

Skin heals more slowly and is more prone to infection. Can wax, but be thorough with aftercare — especially keeping the area clean and watching for irritation that doesn't resolve.
Condition
Pregnancy
Look for: No visual cue — client will disclose.

Can wax. Skin may be more sensitive. Brazilian is common and safe. Positioning may need adjustment in later trimesters — communicate and adjust as needed.
Condition
PCOS / Hormonal Hair Growth
Look for: Noticeably coarser or denser hair than expected. Faster regrowth between appointments.

Adjust expectations on results and regrowth timeline. Consistent waxing every 4 weeks works best. Normal — don't make it a thing.
Condition
Blood Thinners / Aspirin
Look for: Unusual bruising or prolonged redness noted at previous appointments. Client may list medication on the form.

Not a contraindication — just be aware and gentle. Note it if they report unusual bruising afterward.
When to refer out

If something looks unusual, infected, or outside your scope — pause the service and recommend they see a dermatologist before their next appointment. "Let me check on that for you" is always the right first move. Document it on their card.

Language.

Every client interaction — a DM, a text, an email, a conversation at checkout — should sound like one thing: Pynk Nymph. Not whoever is replying. The studio.

You're representing Pynk Nymph — not yourself.

Every reply you send is a signal. Your tone, your words, your pace — they all communicate who the client is talking to. That should always be Pynk Nymph. Not your personal mood, not your individual relationship with that client. The business.

Clients don't experience providers. They experience the studio. Whether they've been coming for two years or just booked their first appointment — they should feel the same warmth, the same directness, the same care every time.

The client's relationship is with the studio — not with you as an individual.
This protects you, protects the brand, and builds something that lasts beyond any one provider.
How This Shows Up Every Day
✓Sounds like Pynk Nymph
  • Warm, direct, and unhurried
  • "We" over "I" — first person plural where possible
  • Consistent across every channel — DM, text, email, in person
  • The same voice at 10am and 6pm
  • When in doubt — keep it simple, kind, and professional
✗Doesn't sound like us
  • "Omg yes bestie!!" — too personal, not brand voice
  • Cold, distant, or defensive replies
  • Escalating a complaint or going back-and-forth
  • Sharing personal opinions about business decisions
  • Responding when you're frustrated — wait, then reply

How to handle this

You don't need to explain why you're rescheduling. Keep it short, offer alternatives, and let them choose. Don't apologize excessively — one acknowledgment is enough. If they can't make the new time, offer to connect them with another available provider.

One important note: if you rescheduled on them and they then need to reschedule the new appointment with little notice — give them grace. They earned it.

"Hey [name], [provider name] needs to reschedule your [service] for [original time]. Would [new day/time] work for you? If not, we can find another time or connect you with [other provider] if you'd prefer."
"Hi [name], we need to move your [service] on [date]. We're sorry for the inconvenience — [new time option] works on our end, or we can look at other availability. What works best for you?"

When there's no script for it

Not every situation comes with a template. When you're unsure what to say, ask yourself three things:

01Does this sound like the business — or like me personally?
02Would I be comfortable if the owner saw this message?
03Am I responding to what the client actually needs — or to how I'm feeling right now?

If any of those feel off — pause. Keep it simple. Or loop in the owner.

When in doubt

If a situation feels tricky — a complaint, a refund request, a difficult client — loop in the owner before you respond. It's always better to say "let me check on that for you" than to handle something incorrectly.

Aftercare.

What clients need to do after their service — and what to tell them before they leave. Know this as well as you know the service itself.

Universal AftercareEvery client, every service
First 24 Hours
No heat
No hot showers, saunas, steam rooms, or intense exercise. Heat = inflammation. The skin is open and needs to calm down first.
First 24 Hours
No friction
No tight clothing, no scratching, no touching the waxed area more than necessary. Let it breathe.
First 48 Hours
No exfoliating
Wait at least 48 hours before using any exfoliant on the waxed area. After that — exfoliate regularly to prevent ingrowns.
Ongoing
Moisturize daily
Hydrated skin waxes better and has fewer ingrowns. Make it a habit, not just post-wax care.
By Service
Brazilian
Brazilian Aftercare
Loose cotton underwear for 24h. No swimming or hot tubs for 48h. Exfoliate at 48h mark. Watch for ingrowns at the bikini line — if they appear, use the ingrown serum, don't pick.
Face
Brow Aftercare
No makeup on the brow area for 24h. Avoid touching or rubbing. Some redness is normal — it should fade within a few hours.
Body
Body Wax Aftercare
Same universal rules apply. Exfoliate at 48h, moisturize daily. Coarse hair areas (back, chest) are more prone to ingrowns — the serum is a good recommendation here.
Tell them, don't assume

Clients don't always read cards. Walk them through the key points verbally — especially "no heat today" and "exfoliate at 48 hours." If they know it from your mouth, they remember it.
